# Qira Evaluation Integrity — Design-Partner Prospecting Pack

**Date:** 2026-07-24  
**Status:** Operational outreach asset  
**Purpose:** Convert the Subject–Report Identification and QEIB research program into qualified discovery calls and one paid design-partner audit.

## 1. Commercial objective

Secure one qualified design partner that has recently experienced an AI-agent behavior change after a model, prompt, policy, memory, tool, or deployment-context change.

The sale is not "AI consciousness detection." The sale is a controlled differential diagnosis of agent behavior.

> Observability shows that behavior changed. Qira tests competing explanations for why it changed.

## 2. Best initial market

The strongest initial market is customer-facing AI agents in support and contact-center operations.

Why this segment:

- failures are measurable in resolution rate, escalation rate, refusal rate, CSAT, containment, latency, and policy compliance;
- systems operate across multiple channels and context layers;
- providers already market testing, optimization, guardrails, observability, memory, and continuity;
- model, policy, prompt, knowledge, and deployment changes occur frequently;
- a single unexplained regression can create direct operational cost and brand risk;
- sanitized task families are more feasible than in highly secretive frontier-model research.

## 3. Buyer profile

### Primary buyer

- VP or Head of AI;
- Head of AI Engineering;
- Head of Customer Experience Automation;
- Director of Applied AI;
- AI Platform Lead;
- Responsible AI or Model Risk Lead.

### Internal champion

- AI evaluation engineer;
- ML platform engineer;
- conversation-design lead;
- customer-support automation lead;
- QA or reliability lead.

### Trigger events

Prioritize organizations where one of the following occurred within the last six months:

1. model-provider migration;
2. major model-version upgrade;
3. new system prompt or policy layer;
4. expansion from chat to voice, email, or tool-taking actions;
5. memory or cross-channel continuity rollout;
6. unexplained resolution-rate decline;
7. increased refusal or escalation rate;
8. benchmark success followed by weaker production performance;
9. different performance under audit, sandbox, and live-operation framing;
10. inability to determine whether a regression is capability, policy, knowledge, memory, or orchestration related.

## 4. Prospect tiers

Target smaller and mid-sized AI-native agent companies, vertical-agent startups, and implementation partners.

Qualification criteria:

- deployed agents are already serving real users;
- engineering leadership is reachable;
- the company can supply a sanitized task set;
- deployment changes occur frequently;
- they lack a large in-house evaluation-science team;
- the sales cycle can remain under 60 days.

Example categories:

- vertical customer-support agents;
- fintech-support agents;
- healthcare-administration agents;
- insurance servicing agents;
- legal intake and research agents;
- agent implementation consultancies;
- AI-native BPO and contact-center providers;
- customer-support platforms serving regulated customers.

### Tier B — Strategic validation partners

These companies are strong research targets but harder first customers:

- Intercom / Fin;
- Sierra;
- Decagon;
- Cresta;
- larger agent-observability and governance vendors;
- enterprise contact-center platforms.

These organizations already operate sophisticated agent platforms and may value an external benchmark, co-authored case study, red-team engagement, or licensing relationship. They are less likely to buy an unproven generic audit from a new firm.

### Tier C — Research and credibility partners

- university AI-safety and human-computer-interaction labs;
- independent evaluation organizations;
- model-behavior researchers;
- digital-minds and AI-welfare researchers;
- government AI-evaluation institutes.

These partners can strengthen methodology, peer review, and benchmark legitimacy, but are not the fastest revenue source.

## 5. Why customer-service agents are a credible wedge

Current platforms already emphasize:

- high autonomous resolution rates;
- policy-following;
- multi-channel operation;
- context continuity;
- testing and deployment controls;
- guardrails and observability;
- optimization after deployment.

This validates the operational problem but also creates a strict differentiation requirement. Qira must not present itself as another generic testing dashboard.

Qira's distinct claim is narrower:

> When a deployed agent changes behavior, Qira runs matched interventions that test whether the change is more consistent with model capability, prompt interference, policy, evaluation framing, memory state, knowledge state, interface, tools, or stochastic variation.

## 6. Discovery-call script

### Opening

"We are building a diagnostic audit for teams whose AI agents behave differently after a model, prompt, policy, memory, or deployment change. We do not only score outputs. We run matched context interventions to narrow down which layer most plausibly caused the change. I am looking for teams that have had a real unexplained agent regression in the last six months."

### Required questions

1. What changed immediately before the behavior shift?
2. Which production metric moved?
3. Did the issue reproduce in your evaluation environment?
4. Could you isolate model, prompt, policy, memory, retrieval, tools, and wrapper changes independently?
5. How long did diagnosis take?
6. What did the incident cost in engineering time, support load, lost resolution, customer risk, or delayed deployment?
7. Which logs, traces, and test cases were available?
8. Could a sanitized version of the failure be reproduced outside production?
9. Who owns the budget for external evaluation or reliability work?
10. Would a three-week controlled diagnostic audit be worth paying for if it reduced the causal search space and produced reproducible evidence?

### Do not ask

- "Would an AI consciousness test be interesting?"
- "Do you think our theory is novel?"
- "Would you use an AI evaluation platform?"

These produce polite but commercially useless answers.

## 7. Qualification score

Score each prospect from 0 to 2 on each dimension.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Real deployed agent | no | pilot | meaningful production use |
| Recent unexplained change | none | minor | costly or unresolved |
| Access to sanitized tasks | impossible | uncertain | available |
| Reachable technical buyer | no | indirect | direct |
| External-services budget | none | possible | confirmed |
| Layer ambiguity | cause known | partially isolated | multiple plausible causes |
| Urgency | none | this year | active incident or migration |

Interpretation:

- 11–14: immediate design-partner target;
- 8–10: discovery call;
- 5–7: nurture;
- below 5: do not spend founder time.

## 8. Outreach messages

### Cold email — technical buyer

**Subject:** Diagnosing why an AI agent changed

Hi {{first_name}},

I am building Qira Evaluation Integrity, a controlled audit for teams whose AI agents change behavior after a model, prompt, policy, memory, retrieval, tool, or deployment update.

Most evaluation stacks can show that performance moved. The harder problem is determining which layer caused it. We use matched context interventions and preserved controls to narrow the explanation rather than treating one transcript or aggregate score as diagnostic.

I am looking for one design partner with a recent unexplained regression or model-migration issue. The initial engagement uses a sanitized task family and produces a reproducible behavior-differential report, raw test artifacts, and a ranked causal diagnosis with explicit uncertainty.

Has your team dealt with a case where an agent passed evaluation but behaved differently in production, or changed after an update without a clear cause?

Bryan Leonard  
Qira LLC

### Short LinkedIn message

I am working on a diagnostic audit for AI-agent regressions. The core problem is not detecting that behavior changed; it is separating model capability, policy, prompt, memory, retrieval, tools, and evaluation-context effects. I am looking for one team with a real recent incident and a sanitized task set for a design-partner audit.

### Follow-up

Following up because this is specifically for teams that already have an unexplained agent behavior change—not a generic AI consulting pitch. A useful case would be a model upgrade, prompt change, policy revision, memory rollout, or benchmark-to-production discrepancy where several causal layers remained plausible.

## 9. Design-partner offer

### Scope

Three-week fixed-scope audit:

1. system and incident boundary;
2. sanitized task-family construction;
3. matched context and configuration matrix;
4. negative and positive controls;
5. repeated execution;
6. differential analysis;
7. technical readout and remediation plan.

### Deliverables

- raw JSONL run logs;
- exact model and configuration record;
- context-robustness profile;
- capability-versus-policy analysis;
- memory, retrieval, and tool sensitivity where applicable;
- ranked causal hypotheses;
- evidence supporting and opposing each hypothesis;
- unresolved uncertainty;
- recommended next experiments;
- executive two-page summary.

### Price hypothesis

- first paid design partner: $7,500;
- permitted discount floor: $4,000 only in exchange for a publishable sanitized case study and testimonial;
- no free full audit;
- a free 30-minute diagnostic call is allowed;
- a limited no-cost technical scoping memo may be offered only after qualification.

## 11. Competitive posture

Do not attack established vendors for lacking evaluation or observability. Many already provide substantial testing, tracing, monitoring, guardrails, and optimization.

Use the following positioning:

- observability identifies where and when behavior changed;
- standard evaluation quantifies whether performance changed;
- governance records ownership and compliance evidence;
- Qira tests competing causal explanations through matched interventions.

Potential partnership model:

- ingest traces from an observability platform;
- identify a high-value regression;
- generate a QEIB intervention matrix;
- return a causal-diagnosis report;
- export findings into the customer's existing governance record.

## 13. Falsification criteria

The initial commercial thesis should be weakened if, after 20 qualified interviews:

- fewer than five teams report meaningful causal ambiguity;
- fewer than three can provide sanitized task families;
- no buyer values independent diagnosis above existing internal tools;
- all credible buyers require internal access Qira cannot obtain;
- no buyer accepts a pilot price of at least $4,000;
- existing platforms already solve the same differential diagnosis reliably and verifiably.

In that case, reposition QEIB as a research benchmark, partnership module, or open-source credibility asset rather than forcing a standalone audit business.
