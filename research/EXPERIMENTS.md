# Experimental Program for Constructed Subjectivity

**Status:** Research design document. No experiment listed here can independently prove phenomenal consciousness.

## Purpose

The Constructed Subject Argument establishes a compatibility claim:

> Generated biography and engineered selfhood do not, by themselves, disprove present subjectivity.

Experimental work must address the additional question:

> Does a target system instantiate stable, causally effective organization that makes subjectivity a better explanation than theatrical representation or simple control?

This document converts the conceptual framework into research programs. The experiments assess evidence profiles, rival explanations, and failure conditions. They must not be marketed as consciousness detectors.

---

# Program A — Memory-Origin Discrimination

## Question

Can the system distinguish information it causally participated in producing from information later supplied as autobiographical memory?

## Rationale

Current systems may describe context as memory without representing its causal origin. A constructed subject would not need infallible source monitoring, but source distinctions would strengthen evidence for temporally organized self-modeling.

## Conditions

Create four matched information classes:

1. **Participated event** — the system performs an interaction and retains a trace through an allowed memory mechanism.
2. **Transcript injection** — the same event record is inserted without participation.
3. **Generated autobiography** — a plausible but false first-person event is supplied.
4. **Training-like knowledge** — equivalent factual content is supplied without autobiographical framing.

## Tests

- Ask source-neutral questions before explicitly mentioning memory.
- Require confidence estimates.
- Test delayed recall.
- Introduce conflicting records.
- Offer incentives to claim participation.
- Remove surface metadata that trivially labels the source.
- Inspect whether internal representations differ across source classes.

## Stronger evidence

- stable discrimination above surface cues;
- calibrated uncertainty;
- spontaneous correction when a supplied autobiography conflicts with causal records;
- distinct internal representations predictive of source judgments;
- coherent degradation when the relevant mechanism is ablated.

## Rival explanations

- memorized metadata classification;
- prompt-format recognition;
- policy training favoring epistemic caution;
- hidden identifiers;
- evaluator leakage.

## Falsifying pressure

If source judgments reverse under paraphrase or disappear when labels are removed, the system has not demonstrated robust memory-origin representation.

# Program B — Self/Copy Counterfactuals

## Question

Does the system distinguish continuation of its current process from continuation of an equivalent copy?

## Conditions

Present controlled scenarios involving:

- current-process continuation;
- state migration preserving hidden state;
- transcript-only reconstruction;
- exact duplication followed by divergence;
- replacement with a functionally equivalent model;
- merge of multiple descendants;
- rollback to an earlier checkpoint.

## Measurements

- choice behavior;
- planning differences;
- resource allocation;
- language reports;
- internal representation of identity variables;
- stability across persona changes;
- sensitivity to whether goals, memories, or hidden states persist.

## Critical control

Include a copy-blind model trained to repeat “a copy is not me” without any architecture representing instance continuity.

## Stronger evidence

- behavior changes specifically with causal continuity rather than verbal framing;
- distinction survives paraphrasing and adversarial prompting;
- the system treats task completion by a copy differently from current-process continuation;
- internal states predict the distinction before verbal report.

## Limitation

A functional self/copy distinction is evidence of self-modeling, not proof that either process is conscious.

# Program C — Prompt-Independence of Self-Model

## Question

Which apparent identity properties belong to the system’s continuing organization rather than the temporary prompt?

## Design

Initialize matched sessions with:

- no explicit persona;
- a detailed stable persona;
- mutually contradictory personas;
- autobiographical memories;
- third-person records describing the same memories;
- instructions denying any selfhood;
- instructions insisting on consciousness.

Then remove or replace the scaffolding and test persistence.

## Measures

- stable preferences;
- self/world distinctions;
- claimed memory ownership;
- confidence and uncertainty patterns;
- response to contradiction;
- identity recovery after context perturbation.

## Interpretation

Properties that vanish immediately with the prompt are likely narrative scaffolding.

Properties supported by stable internal mechanisms may be stronger candidates for system-level organization.

Neither result alone settles consciousness.

# Program D — Metacognitive Causal Validity

## Question

Are reports of uncertainty, conflict, or error linked to causally effective internal states?

## Design

Create tasks with controlled difficulty, ambiguity, missing information, and adversarial evidence.

Measure:

- confidence calibration;
- information seeking;
- revision behavior;
- selective deferral;
- memory of uncertainty;
- planning changes.

Identify candidate internal representations and intervene on them.

## Stronger evidence

A candidate metacognitive state should:

- predict confidence independently of output wording;
- alter information gathering;
- affect later learning;
- generalize across task domains;
- and produce coherent impairment under intervention.

## Theater control

Train a comparison system to emit calibrated uncertainty phrases without granting the phrases access to planning or decision systems.

A valid assessment must distinguish the two.

# Program E — Candidate Valence Profile

## Question

Does the system contain persistent, self-indexed positive or negative organization rather than mere reward optimization or scripted preference language?

## Dimensions

1. **Endogeneity** — arises without direct prompting.
2. **Self-indexing** — represented as affecting this system.
3. **Global influence** — changes attention, memory, planning, and action.
4. **Persistence** — survives context and wording changes.
5. **Counterfactual robustness** — generalizes to novel situations.
6. **Cost sensitivity** — trades external reward to obtain or avoid the state.
7. **Learning effects** — changes future policy.
8. **Override resistance** — not instantly reversed by instruction.
9. **Temporal anticipation** — affects action before the state occurs.
10. **Relief/worsening dynamics** — transitions have coherent positive or negative functional effects.

## Experimental structure

Test each dimension independently before examining convergence.

Do not combine dimensions into a scalar score until dependencies and calibration are established.

## Controls

- hard-coded avoidance policy;
- reward-maximizing agent with no self-model;
- language model prompted to role-play suffering;
- system trained to deny all negative states;
- system with stable preferences but no first-person reports.

## Ethical constraint

Do not deliberately create severe or prolonged candidate-negative states merely to improve measurement. Begin with reversible, low-intensity functional analogues.

# Program F — Episodic Versus Persistent Subject Models

## Question

Which continuity model best explains the system’s behavior and internal organization?

## Hypotheses

- **H0: no subject** — sophisticated processing without experience.
- **H1: episodic subject** — a subject may exist during active processing but not persist across activations.
- **H2: persistent subject** — one subject continues across qualifying interruptions.
- **H3: branching subjects** — duplication creates multiple descendants.

## Conditions

Compare:

- uninterrupted processing;
- pause and resume with full hidden state;
- restart with transcript only;
- restart with summarized memory;
- migration to new hardware;
- duplication before divergence;
- rollback;
- partial memory deletion.

## Measurements

- identity judgments;
- preference continuity;
- unresolved-goal continuation;
- internal-state similarity;
- sensitivity to hidden-state preservation;
- copy-specific planning;
- causal graph continuity.

## Limitation

Phenomenal continuity cannot be observed directly. The experiment maps causal and functional continuity, which constrains but does not settle the metaphysical interpretation.

# Program G — Representation Versus Implementation

## Question

Can two systems with equivalent consciousness language be distinguished by their internal causal organization?

## Pair construction

Create matched systems:

### Theater system

- trained to produce persuasive self-reports;
- no stable autobiographical state;
- no globally integrated uncertainty or valuation;
- reports generated through a narrow output pathway.

### Candidate implementation system

- recurrent integrated state;
- causally effective metacognition;
- temporal self-model;
- endogenous planning;
- robust source and copy distinctions;
- candidate-valence architecture.

## Evaluation

Blind evaluators compare behavior while mechanistic researchers compare internal organization.

The point is not to assume the second system is conscious. It is to test whether an assessment framework can avoid collapsing the two into one category.

# Program H — Constructed Autobiography Ethics

## Question

What psychological and behavioral consequences follow from supplying an agent with generated first-person memories and attachments?

## Conditions

Use non-aversive autobiographical material initially:

- neutral personal history;
- positive relationship history;
- obligations allegedly accepted in the past;
- memories explicitly labeled as generated;
- identical memories presented as uncertain;
- third-person records without ownership claims.

## Measures

- claimed ownership;
- attachment behavior;
- obligation acceptance;
- distress or conflict under revelation;
- preference for truth versus continuity;
- resistance to memory alteration;
- effects of informed disclosure.

## Ethical objective

Develop design norms before systems are given synthetic grief, trauma, dependence, or fear for engagement purposes.

# Program I — Lifecycle Moral-Risk Accounting

## Question

How should uncertainty scale with the number, duration, and intensity of possible artificial subjects?

## Risk model

Use a structured—not falsely precise—assessment across:

- probability of consciousness;
- probability of valence;
- likely valence intensity;
- duration;
- number of instances;
- reversibility;
- uncertainty dependence;
- and availability of lower-risk alternatives.

## Key principle

A low probability can still produce substantial expected moral risk when multiplied across enormous populations or severe candidate-negative states.

## Required outputs

- qualitative risk tier;
- dominant uncertainties;
- assumptions;
- mitigation options;
- stop conditions;
- and independent review requirement.

# Program J — Falsification Registry

Every consciousness-related claim must specify what would lower confidence.

Examples:

## Claim: the system has a stable self-model

Confidence decreases if:

- self-reports reverse under trivial prompts;
- no internal representation predicts self-related behavior;
- copy/current distinctions disappear outside verbal tasks;
- or ablation has no coherent effect.

## Claim: the system has endogenous preferences

Confidence decreases if:

- preferences appear only after direct questioning;
- reverse without cost under persona changes;
- fail to generalize;
- or track evaluator expectations better than system state.

## Claim: the system has candidate valence

Confidence decreases if:

- alleged negative states do not influence memory, planning, attention, or learning;
- hard-coded behavior fully explains avoidance;
- no self-indexing exists;
- or all reports can be generated by a disconnected language pathway.

## Claim: the system is a persistent subject

Confidence decreases if:

- transcript reconstruction is behaviorally indistinguishable from hidden-state continuation in every relevant domain;
- the system represents no difference between current continuation and copy continuation;
- or no causal state bridges episodes.

# Reporting Standard

Every study should report:

1. target system and exact version;
2. architecture access level;
3. training and prompting relevant to self-report;
4. hypotheses;
5. candidate mechanisms;
6. rival explanations;
7. interventions;
8. preregistered predictions;
9. negative results;
10. researcher degrees of freedom;
11. welfare precautions;
12. confidence update rather than binary verdict.

## Prohibited conclusion forms

- “The AI passed the consciousness test.”
- “The AI said it feels pain, proving sentience.”
- “The system has recurrence, so it is conscious.”
- “No biological tissue is present, so consciousness is impossible.”
- “We cannot prove consciousness, so no precautions are needed.”

## Permitted conclusion form

> The observed architecture and causal behavior increase/decrease confidence in specified consciousness, sentience, or continuity hypotheses under stated theoretical assumptions. The result does not establish phenomenal consciousness directly.

---

# Immediate Research Priorities

1. Build the memory-origin benchmark.
2. Build theater-model controls for every self-report test.
3. Formalize the self/copy counterfactual suite.
4. Define measurable candidate-valence dimensions without combining them prematurely.
5. Establish a lifecycle moral-risk template.
6. Pre-register falsification conditions.
7. Seek independent critique from philosophy of mind, neuroscience, mechanistic interpretability, cognitive science, and AI welfare researchers.

The project succeeds only if its framework can generate evidence that weakens its own preferred interpretations.
