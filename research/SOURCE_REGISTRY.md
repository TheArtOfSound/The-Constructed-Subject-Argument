# Source Registry

**Purpose:** Maintain one canonical record of sources used or proposed across the manuscript.

A source appearing here is **not automatically approved for citation**. Each entry must pass three separate checks:

1. **Metadata verification** — author, title, year, venue, DOI or stable URL.
2. **Claim verification** — the source actually supports the exact manuscript claim.
3. **Pinpoint verification** — page, section, proposition, figure, or quoted language is identified where needed.

## Status labels

- **M — Metadata anchor:** bibliographic identity is stable enough for drafting; full text still requires inspection.
- **D — Directly inspected:** full text or official abstract inspected.
- **C — Claim-mapped:** exact manuscript claims mapped to the source.
- **P — Pinpointed:** page or section support recorded.
- **R — Publication-ready:** metadata, claim, pinpoint, and representation checked.
- **X — Rejected:** source is irrelevant, unreliable, secondary where primary is available, or fails to support the claim.

No source should be represented as **R** without human or independent expert confirmation.

# H. Source clusters that must not remain vague

The following manuscript phrases currently refer to literature families and must be replaced by exact sources before publication:

- “recent experimental work”;
- “recent 2026 work”;
- “current AI-welfare research”;
- “work on synthetic lived experience”;
- “session-bound AI-subject proposals”;
- “provenance-grounded agent memory”;
- “current human–AI cognitive-extension research”;
- “recent shutdown-resistance experiments”;
- “peer-preservation behavior”;
- “recent legal scholarship”;
- “digital-mind governance literature.”

Every occurrence must resolve to:

- a verified primary source;
- a date;
- an exact claim;
- and a manuscript citation.

If no suitable primary source exists, the sentence must be rewritten as project speculation or removed.

# Verification workflow

For each source:

1. Open the primary source.
2. Record official metadata.
3. Write a neutral three-sentence summary.
4. List manuscript claims the source appears to support.
5. Copy no more than a short, legally compliant quotation when needed.
6. Record exact section or page.
7. Record important limitations or contrary findings.
8. Mark whether the manuscript currently overstates the result.
9. Assign status M, D, C, P, R, or X.
10. Require independent human confirmation before final publication.

## Governing rule

> A citation is not decoration and not reputational borrowing. It is a checkable dependency between one manuscript sentence and one source.
