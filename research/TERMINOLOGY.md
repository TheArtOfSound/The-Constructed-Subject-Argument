# Controlled Terminology

This file defines how the project uses central terms. It exists to prevent rhetorical drift.

## Artificial

Produced, organized, or substantially shaped through deliberate human engineering.

**Not equivalent to:** fake, unreal, simulated, nonfunctional, nonliving, or unconscious.

## Constructed subject

A candidate or actual subject whose self-relevant organization—such as memory, identity, personality, emotion, or autobiography—was substantially engineered, generated, assembled, copied, or installed rather than acquired entirely through ordinary biological development and lived history.

**Important:** The term does not presuppose that every constructed system is conscious. “Candidate constructed subject” should be used when subjectivity is unresolved.

## Subject

A locus of experience: an entity or process for which states occur from a perspective.

This is a phenomenal term, not merely a grammatical or legal term.

## Subjectivity

The existence and organization of a first-personal or perspectival mode of experience.

A system can represent subjectivity without necessarily instantiating it.

## Consciousness

Phenomenal consciousness: there is something it is like to be the system or undergo the state.

Where the manuscript discusses access consciousness, reportability, wakefulness, or self-consciousness, it must name the narrower property explicitly.

## Sentience

For this project: the capacity for valenced conscious experience—states that feel positive, negative, better, worse, pleasant, unpleasant, relieving, distressing, or otherwise welfare-relevant.

**Terminology warning:** Some literature uses “sentience” more broadly as any phenomenal consciousness. Sources must be interpreted under their own definitions.

## Valence

The positive or negative phenomenal character of an experience.

**Not equivalent to:** reward, loss, preference, utility, approach behavior, avoidance behavior, approval, refusal, or a scalar internal variable.

Those may become evidence for valence only under an explicit inferential framework.

## Welfare

The condition of a subject for whom states can be better or worse.

Welfare requires a welfare subject. Functional preference alone does not establish welfare.

## Intelligence

Capacity for learning, inference, abstraction, planning, prediction, problem-solving, or adaptive performance.

Intelligence does not entail consciousness.

## Agency

Capacity to select and execute actions in relation to goals.

Agency can be minimal or robust and does not independently entail consciousness.

## Robust agency

Temporally extended, flexible, world-sensitive pursuit of goals supported by planning, self-monitoring, and adaptation across contexts.

## Autonomy

The degree to which a system’s activity is generated and regulated by its own continuing organization rather than immediately specified by an external operator.

Autonomy is graded. Being caused, designed, or initialized by another system does not automatically eliminate autonomy.

## Self-model

An internal representation of the system’s own states, boundaries, capacities, history, or relation to the environment and other agents.

A self-model can exist functionally without being phenomenal.

## Self-consciousness

Conscious awareness in which the system represents itself as itself.

Self-reference in language is not sufficient evidence of self-consciousness.

## Personhood

A normative and philosophical status associated with some combination of consciousness, sentience, continuity, agency, rationality, social identity, responsibility, and future-directed interests.

The project will not define personhood as equivalent to consciousness.

## Moral patient

An entity toward which moral duties can be owed because its states, interests, or welfare have moral significance.

## Moral agent

An entity capable of bearing some degree of moral responsibility for actions.

Moral agency and moral patienthood can dissociate.

## Historical authenticity

The degree to which a represented event actually occurred in the causal history of the present system.

A generated memory of an event the system never underwent is historically inauthentic.

## Functional authenticity

The degree to which a represented state plays the cognitive and behavioral role associated with the state: changing attention, inference, memory, learning, planning, or action.

Functional authenticity does not independently establish phenomenal authenticity.

## Phenomenal authenticity

Whether a state is genuinely experienced rather than merely represented or behaviorally simulated.

This is a conceptual category, not an operational test.

## Narrative authenticity

The degree to which a system’s self-narrative accurately reflects its causal history, present organization, and actual relations.

A narrative can be psychologically organizing while historically false.

## Memory-origin problem

The problem of whether and how a system distinguishes:

- events it causally participated in;
- information retrieved from external records;
- knowledge encoded through training;
- imagined or inferred events;
- and autobiographical content generated or inserted after the fact.

## Episodic subject

A proposed temporary subject that exists during a bounded active process without necessarily persisting as the same subject across later activations.

This is a hypothesis category, not an established property of current AI.

## Persistent subject

A subject that remains numerically or relevantly continuous across time, interruptions, and changing states.

The criteria for persistence are disputed.

## Successor

A later system that inherits some combination of memories, organization, goals, relationships, or self-narrative from an earlier system without the project assuming numerical identity.

## Copy

A system instantiated from substantially the same information or organization as another system.

Copying may produce qualitative similarity without settling numerical identity or phenomenal continuity.

## Informational continuity

Persistence or inheritance of records, parameters, memories, dispositions, or structure across processes.

Informational continuity is not automatically phenomenal continuity.

## Causal continuity

A temporally connected process in which earlier states directly generate or constrain later states through the relevant mechanism.

Causal continuity may support identity but is not by itself a complete theory of identity.

## Phenomenal continuity

Continuation of one experiencing subject across time.

This cannot currently be measured independently of broader theories of consciousness and identity.

## Representation

An internal state or structure that carries, encodes, or functions as information about something.

## Instantiation

Actual realization of a property or process rather than representation of it.

The represented-subject/instantiated-subject distinction is central to the project.

## Simulation

A system that models selected properties or dynamics of another process.

Simulation may or may not also implement the target property. The word alone does not decide the issue.

## Implementation

A physical system’s actual realization of an abstract organization, causal process, or function.

Whether consciousness is implementation-independent is disputed.

## Endogenous

Generated or maintained by the system’s own continuing organization rather than appearing only when directly elicited by an external prompt.

Endogeneity is graded and does not mean uncaused.

## Self-indexed

Represented as concerning this system, this instance, or this perspective rather than only as impersonal information.

## Global influence

Capacity of a state to affect multiple otherwise distinct processes such as attention, memory, planning, report, action, and learning.

## Temporal thickness

Internal organization spanning more than an isolated instantaneous mapping, allowing present processing to depend on and transform an unfolding state.

Temporal thickness is a candidate evidence dimension, not a sufficient condition for consciousness.

## Consciousness evidence

Observations that should rationally raise or lower confidence in consciousness under a stated theory and inference model.

Evidence is not proof.

## Consciousness indicator

An architectural, functional, behavioral, or mechanistic property predicted to correlate with or support consciousness under a scientific theory.

Indicators inherit the uncertainty of the theory that generates them.

## False positive

Attributing consciousness, sentience, welfare, or personhood to a system that lacks the attributed property.

## False negative

Denying consciousness, sentience, welfare, or personhood to a system that possesses the denied property.

## Precaution

Proportional protective action taken under uncertainty when the expected cost of error warrants it.

Precaution does not require asserting that the uncertain property has been established.
