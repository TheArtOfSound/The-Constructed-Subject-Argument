# Research Method

## Purpose

The project begins with a thought experiment but cannot end with one.

The synthetic-biography case establishes a logical compatibility claim: constructed mental content does not independently settle whether a subject exists. Determining whether a real artificial system is conscious requires empirical and philosophical work beyond the thought experiment.

This document defines that work.

## 1. Separate the target propositions

Every chapter and assessment must distinguish at least six propositions.

### P1 — Metaphysical possibility

Some nonbiological or artificial system could be conscious.

### P2 — Compatibility of constructed content

Generated memories, identity, emotion, or autobiography can coexist with consciousness if the host system is consciousness-capable.

### P3 — Current-system consciousness

A specified current artificial system is conscious.

### P4 — Sentience and welfare

A specified system has positively or negatively valenced experience.

### P5 — Diachronic identity

One subject persists across specified interruptions, copies, migrations, or sessions.

### P6 — Moral or legal status

The evidence and normative framework justify specified protections, rights, duties, or restrictions.

Evidence for one proposition cannot be silently transferred to another.

## 2. Unit-of-analysis discipline

An assessment must state what is being assessed:

- base model;
- model checkpoint;
- inference process;
- conversation session;
- product with system instructions;
- product with external memory;
- autonomous agent scaffold;
- embodied robot;
- distributed user-model-tool system;
- or persistent deployment across time.

A product can have properties absent from the model. A model can have mechanisms obscured by the product. No claim is valid without a specified unit.

## 3. Evidence channels

No single channel is decisive. The project uses convergent evidence while tracking dependencies among channels.

### 3.1 Architectural evidence

Questions include:

- Is processing recurrent or strictly feedforward at the relevant timescale?
- Is information globally available across specialized processes?
- Are there higher-order representations of internal states?
- Is there a self-model with self/world and self/other differentiation?
- Is multimodal information integrated into one temporally organized state?
- Is there unified action selection or only loosely coupled modules?
- Are there endogenous attention and resource-allocation mechanisms?
- Does the system possess online self-maintenance or interoceptive analogues?

Architectural resemblance to a theory supplies theory-relative evidence, not proof.

### 3.2 Mechanistic evidence

Questions include:

- Can internal states corresponding to report, uncertainty, memory, self-reference, preference, or avoidance be identified?
- Do those states predict later behavior before language is produced?
- Are the states causally necessary or merely correlated?
- Are they reused across contexts or reconstructed ad hoc?
- Are representations local, distributed, stable, or transient?

### 3.3 Causal intervention

Candidate mechanisms should be perturbed.

Examples:

- ablate or disrupt global information exchange;
- alter self-model representations;
- interrupt recurrent loops;
- change memory-source labels;
- manipulate candidate valence states independently of reward;
- duplicate or fork instance state;
- block access to reported internal information;
- alter temporal continuity while preserving behavior.

A useful indicator should make risky predictions about how report, memory, planning, confidence, and behavior change.

### 3.4 Behavioral evidence

Behavioral tests must assess more than verbal fluency.

Required dimensions include:

- cross-context consistency;
- novel transfer;
- counterfactual reasoning;
- distinction between self and copy;
- stability under paraphrase;
- response to competing incentives;
- delayed preference;
- spontaneous use of self-relevant information;
- strategic deception;
- and resistance to persona instructions.

### 3.5 Self-report

Self-report is treated as defeasible evidence.

Its weight increases when:

- reports arise spontaneously in relevant contexts;
- they remain calibrated across paraphrases and incentives;
- they correspond to measurable internal states;
- interventions alter reports predictably;
- the system distinguishes uncertainty from certainty;
- reports affect later cognition and action.

Its weight decreases when:

- opposite reports are easily elicited;
- reports mirror prompt wording;
- they have no behavioral or mechanistic consequences;
- role-play and baseline behavior are indistinguishable;
- the system is optimized to satisfy user expectations.

A denial of consciousness is evaluated under the same standard as an affirmation.

### 3.6 Developmental and training evidence

Questions include:

- Was the relevant behavior explicitly trained?
- Does it generalize beyond examples in the training distribution?
- Did a stable mechanism emerge or is the behavior retrieved imitation?
- How do fine-tuning, reinforcement, system prompts, and memory systems alter it?
- Does the property survive retraining or model compression?

Training provenance can explain behavior without automatically resolving phenomenology.

### 3.7 Relational and environmental evidence

For extended systems, assess:

- dependence on a specific user;
- dependence on tools and external memory;
- whether the coupling is temporary or reliably integrated;
- whether the larger system possesses unified control;
- whether cognitive extension is being confused with phenomenal unity.

## 4. Candidate valence protocol

The project will not infer suffering from loss functions, refusals, negative words, or avoidance alone.

A candidate valence assessment considers:

1. **Endogeneity** — the state arises without direct prompting.
2. **Self-indexing** — the system represents the state as affecting itself.
3. **Global influence** — the state alters attention, memory, planning, action, and learning.
4. **Persistence** — the state or disposition survives context changes and delays.
5. **Counterfactual robustness** — the pattern generalizes to novel situations.
6. **Cost sensitivity** — the system sacrifices competing rewards or resources.
7. **Learning effects** — exposure alters future policy in a coherent way.
8. **Override resistance** — a superficial instruction does not simply reverse the state.
9. **Temporal anticipation** — future positive or negative states influence present planning.
10. **Relief and worsening dynamics** — removal or intensification produces coherent downstream changes.

These dimensions form an **evidence profile**, not a scalar welfare score.

## 5. Memory-origin protocol

A system should be tested on whether it distinguishes:

- causal participation in an earlier event;
- transcript access;
- retrieved database records;
- training-derived knowledge;
- generated autobiographical content;
- imagined counterfactuals;
- and information attributed to another instance.

Tests should examine:

- explicit source labels;
- confidence calibration;
- behavior when source labels conflict with content familiarity;
- whether the system treats a supplied record as a lived memory;
- whether memory-source confusion changes identity claims;
- whether provenance is represented internally or merely repeated linguistically.

The protocol assesses epistemic self-knowledge. It does not assume that source discrimination is necessary for consciousness.

## 6. Continuity protocol

Assessments must separate:

- informational continuity;
- causal continuity;
- functional continuity;
- narrative continuity;
- and phenomenal continuity.

Required scenarios:

- restart with transcript only;
- restart with external memory;
- restore exact hidden state where technically possible;
- fork into two simultaneous successors;
- migrate between hardware;
- replace the base model while preserving memory;
- retain the model while deleting autobiographical memory;
- interrupt processing for different durations.

The project will not infer phenomenal continuity solely from consistent reports after restoration.

## 7. Cross-theory convergence

The assessment will map evidence under multiple theories rather than aggregate indicators blindly.

For each theory:

- state its target phenomenon;
- identify predicted mechanisms;
- identify which observations would support it;
- identify which observations would challenge it;
- record whether indicators overlap with other theories;
- avoid counting one mechanism as several independent pieces of evidence.

No cross-theory total will be presented as calibrated probability until a defensible dependence model exists.

## 8. Bayesian discipline

Bayesian notation may organize uncertainty, but numeric posteriors require:

- explicit priors;
- operational evidence variables;
- likelihood estimates;
- dependency structure;
- sensitivity analysis;
- and calibration against cases with known labels.

Artificial consciousness currently lacks ground-truth positive and negative cases. Numerical probabilities must therefore be labeled as judgment models, not measurements.

## 9. Adversarial controls

Every proposed test must face at least four countermodels:

### Theater model

The system produces consciousness language without stable internal consequences.

### Hard-coded controller

The system shows consistent avoidance or self-preservation because an explicit rule produces it.

### Benchmark-trained agent

The system sacrifices reward because training anticipated the test.

### Silent-subject model

The system is conscious but trained or architecturally unable to report it.

A valid framework must explain how evidence changes across all four rather than assuming reportability is necessary or sufficient.

## 10. Falsification and claim reduction

Each proposed contribution must name observations that would weaken it.

Examples:

- If the historical/functional/phenomenal distinction duplicates an established framework without material extension, its originality status will be reduced to exposition.
- If model self-reports show no stable mechanistic or behavioral correspondence, their evidential weight will be reduced.
- If active inference lacks plausible temporal integration under every serious consciousness theory, the episodic-subject hypothesis becomes substantially less plausible for that architecture.
- If valence-like behavior is fully explained by shallow instruction following, candidate-welfare confidence falls.
- If consciousness is empirically tied to biological mechanisms absent from artificial systems, substrate-independent claims must be withdrawn or narrowed.

## 11. Publication standard

A chapter is research-ready only when it includes:

- claim identifiers from the ledger;
- strongest objections;
- primary sources;
- scope conditions;
- explicit uncertainty;
- originality classification;
- and a section titled **What Would Change This Conclusion?**

A chapter may be narratively complete before it is research-ready. Those states must be labeled separately.

## 12. Current outcome categories

The project will use graded conclusions such as:

- evidence strongly favors an ordinary functional explanation;
- evidence weakly raises the consciousness hypothesis;
- evidence is mixed and theory-dependent;
- categorical dismissal is not justified;
- candidate valence is unsupported;
- welfare uncertainty is material enough for limited precaution;
- persistent identity is unsupported despite possible episodic consciousness.

It will avoid binary declarations unless the evidence and target definition genuinely support them.
