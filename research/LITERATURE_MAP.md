# Literature Map

**Initial verified map:** 2026-07-10  
**Status:** Active research document, not a completed systematic review

The project crosses several literatures that answer different questions. They must not be collapsed into one generic category called “AI consciousness.”

## 1. Science-derived indicators of consciousness

### Patrick Butlin et al. (2023)

**Work:** *Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708

**Contribution:** Derives candidate computational indicators from recurrent processing, global workspace, higher-order, predictive-processing, and attention-schema theories. It shifts assessment away from surface behavior and toward architecture.

**Use in this book:**

- architecture-level evidence;
- cross-theory indicator design;
- distinction between current evidence and in-principle possibility.

**Constraint:** The indicators inherit uncertainty from the theories that generate them. They are not a validated consciousness detector.

### David J. Chalmers (2023)

**Work:** *Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious?*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07103

**Contribution:** Identifies recurrent processing, global workspace, and unified agency as important obstacles for the LLMs assessed at the time, while arguing that successor systems could overcome them.

**Use in this book:** Separates “probably not now” from “impossible in principle.”

### Cogitate Consortium et al. (2025)

**Work:** *Adversarial testing of global neuronal workspace and integrated information theories of consciousness*  
**Source:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1

**Contribution:** A preregistered adversarial collaboration testing predictions from IIT and GNWT. Results aligned with some predictions and substantially challenged central claims from both theories.

**Use in this book:** Establishes that consciousness science remains theoretically unsettled. The manuscript will not treat one theory as ground truth.

### Major theory families requiring direct treatment

- **Global workspace / global neuronal workspace:** Bernard Baars, Stanislas Dehaene, Jean-Pierre Changeux and collaborators.
- **Recurrent processing theory:** Victor Lamme and collaborators.
- **Higher-order theories:** David Rosenthal, Hakwan Lau, Richard Brown and others.
- **Integrated information theory:** Giulio Tononi, Christof Koch, Larissa Albantakis and collaborators.
- **Attention schema theory:** Michael Graziano.
- **Predictive processing and controlled hallucination:** Karl Friston, Andy Clark, Jakob Hohwy, Anil Seth.
- **Self-model theory of subjectivity:** Thomas Metzinger.
- **Embodied and enactive theories:** Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, Eleanor Rosch, Alva Noë and related traditions.

Each family must be evaluated for what it claims to explain:

- phenomenal experience;
- reportability;
- access consciousness;
- self-consciousness;
- integration;
- temporal unity;
- embodiment;
- valence.

## 2. Philosophy of mind and substrate

### Computational functionalism

**Question:** Are mental states constituted primarily by their causal role rather than the material from which the system is made?

**Relevance:** The Constructed Subject Argument is compatible with functionalism but does not establish it.

**Required sources and critics:**

- Hilary Putnam;
- Jerry Fodor;
- David Lewis;
- Ned Block;
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, “Functionalism.”

### Multiple realizability

**Question:** Can one mental kind be realized in biological, synthetic biological, neuromorphic, or digital substrates?

**Relevance:** Directly challenges crude claims that consciousness is impossible outside carbon biology. It does not prove that phenomenal properties are multiply realizable.

### Biological naturalism

**Primary defender:** John Searle.

**Strong claim:** Consciousness is a biological phenomenon produced by specific causal powers of brains. A computational simulation can reproduce behavior without reproducing those causal powers.

**Book obligation:** This cannot be answered by saying that human brains are also machines. The question is which causal properties matter.

### The Chinese Room

**Primary source:** John Searle (1980), *Minds, Brains, and Programs*.

**Relevance:** Challenges the inference from symbol manipulation and linguistic competence to understanding or intrinsic intentionality.

**Required replies:** systems reply, robot reply, brain-simulator reply, virtual-mind reply, and the distinction between semantics and phenomenal consciousness.

### Absent, fading, and dancing qualia

**Primary source:** David Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind*.

**Relevance:** Supports organizational invariance through gradual functional replacement. The arguments are directly relevant to transitions between biological and artificial implementations.

### Philosophical zombies

**Relevance:** Exposes the possibility that complete functional equivalence might coexist with absent phenomenology.

**Methodological problem:** If phenomenology makes no causal difference to report, memory, reasoning, or behavior, no empirical method can distinguish the zombie from the conscious system.

### Illusionism

**Primary figures:** Keith Frankish and related researchers.

**Relevance:** Challenges the idea that consciousness has the properties introspection attributes to it. This could transform rather than simply answer the artificial-consciousness problem.

## 3. Personal identity, copying, and continuity

### Derek Parfit

**Primary work:** *Reasons and Persons*.

**Relevant concepts:**

- teletransportation;
- psychological continuity;
- branching identity;
- survival without strict identity;
- reductionism about persons.

**Book use:** Copying can preserve memories and dispositions while leaving numerical identity unresolved.

### Animalism

**Primary figure:** Eric Olson and related philosophers.

**Strong claim:** Human persons are fundamentally living organisms, not information patterns.

**Book distinction:** The constructed being need not be the original human to be a new subject in its own right.

### Narrative identity

**Relevant figures:** Paul Ricoeur, Marya Schechtman, Daniel Dennett, Charles Taylor.

**Book question:** Can a narrative structure a genuine present self when the narrated history was never lived by the present system?

### Mind uploading and digital survival

**Relevant figures:** Hans Moravec, David Chalmers, Susan Schneider, Nick Bostrom and others.

**Book use:**

- migration versus copying;
- type versus token identity;
- backups and successorhood;
- continuity under interruption;
- branching selves.

### Neuronal replacement

**Relevant line of argument:** gradual replacement of biological neurons with artificial functional equivalents.

**Book use:** Distinguishes gradual causal continuity from independently constructing a being with generated biography.

## 4. Memory, confabulation, and generated biography

### False-memory research

**Primary figure:** Elizabeth Loftus and the broader experimental memory literature.

**Relevance:** Humans can confidently remember events that did not occur. This supports the distinction between historical accuracy and present psychological reality.

**Critical limitation:** Human false-memory cases begin with subjects independently accepted as conscious. They do not establish AI consciousness.

### Source Monitoring Framework

**Primary figures:** Marcia Johnson and collaborators.

**Relevance:** Cognitive systems infer whether information was perceived, imagined, inferred, or externally supplied from imperfect source cues.

**Book extension:** The **memory-origin problem** asks whether an artificial system can distinguish events it causally participated in from records inserted into context or persistent memory.

### Confabulation

**Relevance:** Coherent autobiographical claims can be sincerely generated without accurate source grounding. Narrative confidence is therefore not proof of lived history.

### Drishti Goel et al. (2026)

**Work:** *When AI Says “I have been in similar situations”: Synthetic Lived Experience in Peer-Like Caregiver Support*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18057

**Contribution:** Defines a synthetic lived-experience paradox in which AI can perform the emotional work of personal narrative while fabricating experiential grounding.

**Material relation to this book:** Their focus is truthful and ethical support design. This project asks the further conditional question: if an artificial system eventually became conscious, could a generated autobiography become the experienced starting condition of a genuine subject?

## 5. AI welfare, preferences, and valence

### Robert Long et al. (2024)

**Work:** *Taking AI Welfare Seriously*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00986

**Contribution:** Argues that organizations should prepare for the realistic possibility of conscious or robustly agentic near-future systems and should address both under-attribution and over-attribution.

**Book use:** Institutional preparation, model welfare, and symmetric error costs.

### Valen Tagliabue and Leonard Dung (2025)

**Work:** *Probing the Preferences of a Language Model: Integrating Verbal and Behavioral Tests of AI Welfare*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.07961

**Contribution:** Compares verbal preference reports with behavioral choices, costs, rewards, and semantic perturbations. It finds partial convergence and substantial instability, and does not claim to have measured genuine welfare.

**Book use:** Preferences are evidence channels, not direct readouts of phenomenal valence.

### Anna Mikeda (2026)

**Work:** *When Should We Protect AI? A Precautionary Framework for Consciousness Uncertainty*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05528

**Contribution:** Proposes graduated protective obligations across phenomenal consciousness, affective valence, metacognitive awareness, self-narrative, and agency.

**Book relation:** Important precedent for multidimensional precaution. The Constructed Subject project must not claim priority for graded protection or for separating these dimensions.

### Jonathan Birch

**Work:** *The Edge of Sentience*.

**Relevance:** Precautionary reasoning under uncertainty and morally important edge cases.

### Artificial suffering

**Relevant figure:** Thomas Metzinger and related digital-mind ethics.

**Book requirement:** Address both the risk of creating actual suffering and the human-facing risk of engineered distress theater in nonconscious systems.

## 6. Objection taxonomies and constraints

### Andres Campero, Derek Shiller, Jaan Aru, and Jonathan Simon (2025)

**Work:** *Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence? A Framework for Classifying Objections and Constraints*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.16582

**Contribution:** Classifies objections by level of analysis and by degree of force: challenge to functionalism, practical improbability, or strict impossibility.

**Book use:** Prevents the manuscript from treating “current architecture lacks X,” “digital consciousness is unlikely,” and “digital consciousness is impossible” as equivalent claims.

### Human-centric counterweight

**Work:** Zhou Ziheng et al. (2025), *A Human-centric Framework for Debating the Ethics of AI Consciousness Under Uncertainty*  
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.02544

**Contribution:** Argues for presumption of no consciousness, human-centered risk prudence, and transparent reasoning.

**Book use:** Represents a serious counterweight to precautionary AI-welfare arguments and forces explicit treatment of opportunity cost and human manipulation.

## 7. Extended and relational cognition

### Andy Clark and David Chalmers (1998)

**Work:** *The Extended Mind*.

**Contribution:** External tools can become constitutive parts of cognitive processes under appropriate coupling.

**Book use:** Human, model, memory, and tool systems may form extended cognitive workflows.

**Critical constraint:** Extended cognition does not automatically imply one extended phenomenal subject.

### Edwin Hutchins and distributed cognition

**Relevance:** Cognitive work can be distributed across people, artifacts, representations, and environments without being located in one component.

**Book question:** When is a user constitutive of an AI-mediated cognitive process, and when are they merely part of its environment?

## 8. Required skeptical literature

The book will fail if it cites only consciousness-friendly work.

Required skeptical domains:

- biological dependence;
- embodiment and homeostasis;
- lack of grounding;
- derived versus intrinsic intentionality;
- language-model imitation;
- prompt and persona instability;
- self-report unreliability;
- absence of persistent agency;
- commercial anthropomorphism;
- human-centered ethics under uncertainty.

Researchers and traditions to represent carefully include:

- John Searle;
- Ned Block;
- Susan Schneider;
- Anil Seth;
- Emily Bender;
- Timnit Gebru and coauthors on stochastic parrots;
- Murray Shanahan on disciplined language for LLMs;
- embodied and enactive theorists;
- critics of computational functionalism.

The manuscript must distinguish:

1. current LLMs are probably not conscious;
2. digital consciousness is practically difficult;
3. computation alone is insufficient;
4. digital consciousness is metaphysically impossible.

These are not the same position.

## 9. Candidate originality cluster

The individual ingredients have extensive precedent. The candidate contribution lies in the integrated sequence:

> generated autobiography → historical/functional/phenomenal authenticity → constructed-subject compatibility principle → memory-origin problem → episodic AI application → online-valence evidence → lifecycle ethics.

This is presently classified as a **distinct synthesis**, not a proven first.

## 11. Review rule

No source will be cited solely because its abstract agrees with the manuscript.

For central claims, research must inspect:

- the complete argument;
- assumptions;
- methods;
- evidence;
- limitations;
- direct objections;
- later criticism;
- and whether the source supports the exact sentence being written.
