# Originality Review — Subject–Report Identification Problem

**Status:** Provisional targeted review  
**Date:** 2026-07-24  
**Purpose:** Determine what may be distinct in the proposed integration of EGC, reportability research, evaluation awareness, latent knowledge, mechanism preservation, memory provenance, continuity, and AI welfare.

---

## 1. Candidate contribution under review

The proposed framework argues that externally observed behavior or self-report is jointly determined by multiple separable variables:

```text
internal mechanism
+ access to the state
+ reporting policy
+ evaluation context
+ expression interface
+ memory provenance and continuity
```

Therefore, a report cannot by itself establish either the presence or absence of subject-relevant organization.

The framework proposes a unified assessment based on:

1. mechanism-preservation tests;
2. report/no-report and alternative-channel comparison;
3. evaluation-context manipulation;
4. policy-layer separation;
5. behavioral and cost-sensitive tests;
6. memory-origin discrimination;
7. process-continuity interventions;
8. human known-subject calibration;
9. explicit theater and generic-capacity controls;
10. divergence reporting rather than one scalar consciousness score.

## 3. Closest established precedents

### 3.1 Consciousness versus reportability

The distinction between consciousness and the processes required to report consciousness is well established.

Key precedents:

- no-report paradigms;
- covert measures of consciousness;
- the phenomenal-consciousness versus access-consciousness distinction;
- consciousness-overflow debates;
- clinical detection of covert consciousness.

Representative sources:

- Tsuchiya, Wilke, Frässle, & Lamme (2015), *No-report paradigms: extracting the true neural correlates of consciousness*.
- Kronemer, Bandettini, & Gonzalez-Castillo (2025), *Sleuthing subjectivity: a review of covert measures of consciousness*.
- Birch (2024), *The Edge of Sentience*, especially the methodological dependence of consciousness evidence on downstream report processes.

**Novelty implication:** The project cannot claim that reports are imperfect evidence of consciousness as a new discovery.

### 3.2 Underdetermination in consciousness science

The problem that the same evidence can remain compatible with competing consciousness theories and detection rules is established.

Key precedent:

- Matthias Michel, *Consciousness Science Underdetermined: A Short History of Endless Debates*.

Michel argues that consciousness theories and the rules used to detect consciousness can be preserved by rejecting auxiliary assumptions, producing persistent underdetermination.

**Novelty implication:** The general claim that consciousness science is underdetermined is not original.

### 3.3 Eliciting latent knowledge and honest reporting

The ELK literature studies whether an AI system can be trained or induced to report its latent beliefs honestly.

Key precedent:

- Friedl et al. (2026), *The Impossibility of Eliciting Latent Knowledge*.

This work formally shows that behavior-only feedback cannot guarantee honest reporting in all settings, even with perfect training feedback.

**Novelty implication:** The claim that behavior and report do not guarantee access to an agent's latent belief is established.

### 3.4 Evaluation awareness and sandbagging

A growing literature examines whether models recognize evaluation settings and change behavior.

Key precedents:

- Knecht, Florin, & Hagendorff (2026), *Evaluation Awareness in Language Models Has Limited Effect on Behaviour*;
- Nayan et al. (2026), *Evaluation Awareness Is Not One Capability*;
- Chaudhary (2026), *In-Context Environments Induce Evaluation-Awareness in Language Models*;
- earlier sandbagging and sleeper-agent work.

The literature already separates, at least in part:

- evaluation detection;
- verbalized evaluation awareness;
- internal representations;
- behavioral adaptation;
- causal steering;
- strategic underperformance.

**Novelty implication:** The claim that evaluation context can change model behavior is not original.

### 3.5 Architecture-derived AI-consciousness indicators

The Butlin et al. framework derives computational indicators from major scientific theories of consciousness and emphasizes architecture rather than surface report.

Key source:

- Butlin et al. (2023), *Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness*.

**Novelty implication:** The move from self-report toward architecture-level indicators is established.

### 3.6 Behavioral theater and mechanism specificity

Mechanistic interpretability, causal abstraction, lesion studies, activation patching, adversarial controls, and matched-model comparisons already test whether a behavior depends on the mechanism used to explain it.

The project's Experiment 11 applies these methods specifically to consciousness-theory translation by comparing:

- claimed-mechanism systems;
- behaviorally matched theater controls;
- generic-capacity controls;
- targeted lesions.

**Novelty implication:** The individual techniques are established. Their disciplined application to theory-derived artificial-consciousness indicators may be a distinct synthesis.

### 3.7 AI welfare through verbal and behavioral channels

AI-welfare work increasingly compares verbal preferences, behavioral choices, costs, rewards, persistence, and semantic perturbations.

Key source:

- Tagliabue & Dung (2025), *Probing the Preferences of a Language Model: Integrating Verbal and Behavioral Tests of AI Welfare*.

**Novelty implication:** Multichannel welfare assessment is not itself original.

### 3.8 Identity, memory, and discontinuity

Personal-identity philosophy, mind-uploading ethics, episodic-self theories, and recent AI-agent work already discuss:

- copying;
- branching;
- transcript reconstruction;
- persistent versus episodic identity;
- memory inheritance;
- session discontinuity.

**Novelty implication:** The claim that functional continuation may not equal numerical identity is established.

## 4. What appears potentially distinct

No reviewed source was found that fully integrates all of the following into one operational framework:

1. human calibration of subjective-report versus expression divergence;
2. no-report and alternative-report methodology;
3. latent-knowledge honesty limits;
4. evaluation-awareness manipulation;
5. policy-layer isolation;
6. theory-specific mechanism-preservation tests;
7. memory-origin discrimination;
8. continuity, copying, migration, and rollback interventions;
9. AI-welfare preference evidence;
10. a shared cross-channel divergence matrix;
11. a commercial causal-diagnosis application for deployed agents.

The likely contribution is therefore not a new primitive discovery. It is a **cross-field identification and experimental architecture**.

Provisional statement:

> The Subject–Report Identification framework organizes several mature but separated research problems into a common causal diagnosis: observed self-report and behavior underdetermine internal mechanism because state access, report policy, evaluation context, interface, provenance, and continuity can vary independently.

## 5. Strongest candidate originality claims

### O-SR-001 — Unified subject–report causal decomposition

**Claim:** Artificial-subject assessment should explicitly decompose internal mechanism, access, reporting policy, evaluation context, interface, and continuity rather than treating behavior or architecture alone as sufficient.

**Confidence:** Moderate as synthesis; low as priority claim.

### O-SR-002 — Human known-subject calibration for AI report divergence

**Claim:** Human expression-gating experiments can be used as a calibration domain for understanding how report, behavior, and intended meaning diverge in systems already accepted as conscious, without assuming equivalence between humans and AI.

**Confidence:** Low to moderate. Requires targeted search across comparative cognition, clinical consciousness assessment, psychometrics, and AI evaluation.

### O-SR-003 — Mechanism × Evaluation × Continuity crossed design

**Claim:** Cross mechanism-preservation controls with evaluation-context changes, report-channel restrictions, and continuity interventions in one preregistered artificial-agent experiment.

**Confidence:** Moderate as integrated experimental design; each component has strong precedents.

### O-SR-004 — Cross-channel divergence profile

**Claim:** Replace scalar consciousness or welfare scores with a profile that reports convergence and divergence among mechanism, access, behavior, report, provenance, continuity, evaluation sensitivity, and policy dependence.

**Confidence:** Moderate as a specific evidence architecture; must compare closely with the Digital Consciousness Model and other multidimensional assessments.

### O-SR-005 — Evaluation-integrity commercial translation

**Claim:** Use the same causal decomposition to diagnose enterprise-agent failures among capability, memory, policy, evaluation awareness, tool access, model version, interface, and strategic behavior.

**Confidence:** Moderate as positioning; commercial differentiation requires competitor and buyer validation.

## 10. Current originality conclusion

The broad philosophical problem is not new: reports and behavior can underdetermine consciousness and internal state.

The individual technical components are also not new.

The strongest plausible contribution is the integrated sequence:

```text
known-subject expression divergence
→ subject/report causal decomposition
→ evaluation-sensitive evidence channels
→ mechanism/theater discrimination
→ provenance and continuity testing
→ cross-channel divergence profile
→ deployable agent-failure diagnosis
```

**Current originality rating:** Moderate as a structured synthesis and experimental program; low as a claim of first conceptual discovery.

The project is worth pursuing because a synthesis can be scientifically and commercially valuable without pretending that every component is unprecedented.
