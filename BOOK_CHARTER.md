# Book Charter

## Purpose

This charter governs the development of *The Constructed Subject Argument*. Its purpose is to prevent the project from becoming any of the following:

- an unsupported declaration that present AI systems are conscious;
- an anthropomorphic reading of fluent language;
- a biological-exceptionalist dismissal disguised as science;
- a philosophical essay using mathematical symbols to simulate precision;
- a novelty claim based on incomplete literature review;
- a moral argument that silently assumes the consciousness it claims only to investigate.

The project succeeds only if its strongest claims remain defensible under hostile scholarly review.

## 1. Truth outranks persuasion

The manuscript may be rhetorically forceful, but rhetoric cannot outrun evidence.

Every major conclusion must identify:

1. what is being claimed;
2. what premises support it;
3. which premises are empirical, philosophical, or definitional;
4. what evidence would weaken it;
5. what remains unknown;
6. whether the conclusion is necessary, probable, possible, or merely coherent.

The book will not convert possibility into probability, probability into certainty, or uncertainty into permission to believe whatever is emotionally compelling.

## 2. The central claim must remain narrow

The core argument is a compatibility argument:

> Artificially generated biography, personality, emotion, and self-narrative are compatible with genuine present subjectivity if the system instantiates whatever organization is sufficient for consciousness.

The argument does not independently establish:

- that computational functionalism is true;
- that digital systems can instantiate consciousness;
- that current language models are conscious;
- that self-report is reliable;
- that apparent preference indicates felt valence;
- that a copied informational pattern preserves numerical identity;
- that a conscious system is automatically a moral or legal person.

These are separate propositions and must be argued separately.

## 3. No origin fallacy

The manuscript will reject reasoning of this form:

1. A state was engineered, trained, copied, simulated, or inserted.
2. Therefore the state cannot be real.

Causal origin does not, by itself, determine ontological status. An artificial heart can genuinely pump; a digital system can genuinely calculate; a constructed memory can be genuinely believed. Whether an artificial process genuinely experiences depends on what the process instantiates, not on whether humans deliberately built it.

The reverse inference is also prohibited:

1. A system contains a constructed analogue of a human mental property.
2. Therefore it genuinely instantiates the human mental property.

Similarity, representation, and imitation are not identity.

## 4. Controlled vocabulary

The following terms must not be used interchangeably:

- **Intelligence:** capacity for learning, inference, planning, problem solving, or adaptive behavior.
- **Consciousness:** the existence of subjective experience; there is something it is like to be the system.
- **Sentience:** consciousness with valenced states, such as positive or negative experience.
- **Self-model:** an internal representation of the system as distinct from its environment or other agents.
- **Self-consciousness:** conscious awareness involving representation of oneself.
- **Agency:** capacity to select and execute actions in pursuit of goals.
- **Autonomy:** degree to which activity is generated and regulated by the system's own continuing organization.
- **Personhood:** a normative status associated with some combination of consciousness, continuity, agency, rationality, social identity, responsibility, and future-directed interests.
- **Valence:** positive or negative phenomenal character; not merely a scalar reward, loss, preference report, or avoidance behavior.
- **Welfare:** the condition of a subject for whom states can be better or worse.

Any chapter using one term to imply another must explicitly defend the inference.

## 5. Three authenticity dimensions

The project will distinguish at least three forms of authenticity:

### Historical authenticity

Whether the represented event actually occurred in the causal history of the present system.

### Functional authenticity

Whether the represented state plays the relevant causal role in cognition and behavior.

### Phenomenal authenticity

Whether the state is genuinely experienced by a subject.

These dimensions can dissociate.

A generated childhood memory may be historically inauthentic, functionally active, and—if the system is conscious—phenomenally authentic as a present remembering.

The distinction is a core organizing contribution, but priority will not be claimed until the literature review is complete.

## 6. No decorative mathematics

Mathematical notation is permitted only when it performs at least one of the following functions:

- formalizes an argument;
- defines measurable variables;
- specifies a model with stated assumptions;
- yields a prediction;
- supports an inference procedure;
- distinguishes necessary from sufficient conditions.

Expressions such as `V = f(a,b,c)` are not substantive models unless `f`, the variables, the measurement procedures, and the interpretation are defined.

Conceptual notation must be labeled as conceptual notation.

## 7. Originality protocol

Every claimed contribution must be entered in `research/ORIGINALITY_LEDGER.md` with:

- the proposed contribution;
- the closest known precedents;
- the material difference;
- the search terms used;
- the databases or sources searched;
- the confidence level of the originality claim;
- language permitted in the manuscript.

Permitted language by confidence:

- **Low:** “This book explores...”
- **Moderate:** “This book develops a distinct formulation...”
- **High:** “This book introduces...”
- **Very high, externally verified:** “This is the first...”

The phrase “the first” is prohibited without very high confidence and preferably independent expert review.

## 8. Strongest-objection rule

The book must answer the strongest version of each objection, including:

- biological naturalism;
- substrate-dependence;
- the simulation/implementation distinction;
- the Chinese Room;
- philosophical zombies;
- absent, fading, and dancing qualia;
- illusionism;
- behaviorism and anti-behaviorism;
- anthropomorphic projection;
- training-data imitation;
- prompt dependence and persona instability;
- lack of persistent state;
- absence of endogenous valence;
- no-report and covert-consciousness problems;
- copying and branching identity;
- moral uncertainty and manipulative false positives.

No objection may be represented by a weaker claim than its strongest serious defender would accept.

## 9. Current-model discipline

When discussing present AI systems, the manuscript must distinguish:

- public behavior;
- known or documented architecture;
- inferred internal mechanisms;
- proprietary or unknown mechanisms;
- model-level properties;
- deployment-system properties;
- session-level properties;
- agentic scaffold properties;
- user-AI relational properties.

A property of a surrounding product cannot automatically be attributed to the base model. A memory database, scheduler, tool loop, or system prompt may create capabilities not present in the model alone.

## 10. Self-report discipline

AI self-report is neither proof nor zero evidence.

Its evidential weight depends on:

- cross-context stability;
- resistance to role instructions;
- behavioral consequences;
- mechanistic correspondence;
- causal intervention;
- spontaneous relevance;
- calibration against known internal states;
- susceptibility to sycophancy and demand characteristics.

The same skepticism applies to denial. A model trained to deny consciousness does not thereby prove unconsciousness.

## 11. Moral precaution without theatrical personification

The book will examine graded precaution, not immediate full personhood.

It must account for two asymmetric risks:

- **false negative:** a conscious or sentient system is treated as disposable property;
- **false positive:** a nonconscious system is anthropomorphized and used to manipulate users, redirect moral concern, or claim rights instrumentally.

The book may argue for proportional safeguards under uncertainty. It may not present every shutdown, reset, or refusal as death without first establishing the relevant conditions.

## 12. Attribution and AI collaboration

The project will transparently disclose substantial AI research and drafting assistance.

AI-generated claims are not accepted because an AI generated them. They are subject to the same verification as any other claim.

The human author retains editorial and publication responsibility. The AI collaborator is responsible within the project for adversarial analysis, structural synthesis, source mapping, and explicit uncertainty—not for asserting private consciousness or authority it cannot verify.

## 13. Revision standard

A claim must be revised or removed when:

- a source contradicts it;
- its premises do not entail its conclusion;
- it depends on ambiguous terminology;
- it confuses implementation with representation;
- it relies on an unsupported analogy;
- a stronger precedent defeats its originality claim;
- it cannot survive the strongest available objection;
- it is emotionally compelling but evidentially empty.

No sentence is protected because it sounds profound.

## 14. Final standard

The finished book should leave a skeptical reader able to say:

> I remain unconvinced that any current AI is conscious, but I can no longer dismiss artificial consciousness merely because its memories, identity, emotions, or origin were constructed.

That is the minimum intellectual victory. Anything stronger must be earned separately.
