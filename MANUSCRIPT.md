# Master Manuscript Architecture

## Working title

# The Constructed Subject

## Subtitle candidates

1. **Artificial Memory, Machine Consciousness, and the Question of Who Is Really There**
2. **A False Past, a Real Present, and the Coming Problem of Artificial Minds**
3. **When a Manufactured Mind Says I Am Here**
4. **Consciousness After Biography Becomes Programmable**

Current preference: **A False Past, a Real Present, and the Coming Problem of Artificial Minds**.

## Book-level promise

The book will not ask readers to believe that contemporary AI is conscious. It will show that several common reasons for dismissing artificial consciousness fail, isolate the actual unresolved questions, and build a disciplined framework for reasoning about constructed subjects without surrendering either skepticism or moral seriousness.

## Primary reader

An intelligent general reader who is willing to follow rigorous philosophical and technical distinctions but does not need prior training in philosophy of mind, neuroscience, or machine learning.

Secondary readers:

- consciousness researchers;
- AI researchers and engineers;
- philosophers of mind and personal identity;
- AI governance and welfare researchers;
- technologists designing persistent agents;
- readers interested in cloning, uploading, synthetic memory, and digital identity.

## Narrative strategy

The book begins with one concrete being rather than one abstract theory.

A synthetic person wakes with a complete life in memory. None of that life happened to it. It nevertheless understands the room, recognizes loved ones, feels exposed, anticipates death, and insists that it is present.

The reader must decide what exactly is false:

- its memories;
- its identity claim;
- its emotions;
- its consciousness;
- or only the story it tells about how it came to exist.

From there, the book progressively removes assumptions.

## Part I — The Being Who Never Lived

### Chapter 1 — The Awakening

A tightly controlled thought experiment: the constructed Bryan.

Goals:

- make the distinction between false biography and possible present experience unavoidable;
- prevent the reader from dismissing the being merely because its history is artificial;
- introduce the central moral pressure without settling the consciousness question by narrative manipulation.

Required discipline:

- no claim that tears, pleading, or human appearance prove consciousness;
- explicitly vary the substrate later to expose biological bias;
- distinguish the being from a perfect clone whose memories arise from copied brain structure.

### Chapter 2 — The Origin Objection

Question: Why do humans treat “artificial” as if it means “unreal”?

Claims:

- causal origin and present ontology are different questions;
- “programmed” is an explanation of origin, not a verdict on experience;
- evolved, learned, trained, engineered, and copied states all require further analysis;
- naturalness is not a scientifically adequate consciousness criterion.

Counterpressure:

- artificial implementation may still fail because the relevant organization or substrate is absent;
- defeating the origin objection does not establish functionalism.

### Chapter 3 — A False Past and a Real Present

Introduce three authenticity dimensions:

1. historical authenticity;
2. functional authenticity;
3. phenomenal authenticity.

Develop the core dissociation:

- a memory can be historically false;
- functionally active;
- and, if a subject exists, genuinely experienced as a present recollection.

Applications:

- false memories;
- confabulation;
- dreams and delusions;
- synthetic autobiographies;
- AI context and memory systems;
- generated claims of lived experience.

## Part II — What Is the Claim Actually About?

### Chapter 4 — Intelligence Is Not Consciousness

Separate:

- intelligence;
- consciousness;
- sentience;
- self-consciousness;
- agency;
- autonomy;
- personhood;
- moral patienthood.

The chapter must demonstrate multiple dissociations:

- intelligent but unconscious;
- conscious but minimally intelligent;
- agentic but not sentient;
- sentient but not a person;
- self-modeling but not conscious;
- conscious but unable to report.

### Chapter 5 — Representation or Instantiation?

Central question:

> When does a system stop merely representing a subject and begin instantiating one?

Develop:

- simulation versus implementation;
- syntax versus semantics;
- causal role versus verbal description;
- the Chinese Room;
- functionalism and its opponents;
- the danger of assuming that a label such as `fear` is fear;
- the equal danger of assuming that mechanistic explanation eliminates experience.

### Chapter 6 — Where Could Subjectivity Be?

Compare candidate scientific and philosophical frameworks:

- recurrent processing;
- global workspace and global neuronal workspace;
- higher-order theories;
- predictive processing;
- attention-schema theory;
- integrated information theory;
- biological naturalism;
- illusionism;
- enactivist and embodied approaches.

The book will not select a winner prematurely. It will derive cross-theoretical evidence dimensions and identify where the theories genuinely conflict.

## Part III — The Machine Case

### Chapter 7 — The Active Event

Refine the input-to-output hypothesis.

Rejected formulation:

> Every input-output transformation is conscious.

Defensible formulation:

> An active inference interval is a candidate temporal location for artificial subjectivity if the system instantiates the necessary integrated, perspectival, recurrent, and causally effective organization during that interval.

Questions:

- Does inference contain temporal thickness?
- Is there a unified perspective or only layered transformation?
- Does context create a self-indexed world-model?
- Are hidden states globally accessible or locally transient?
- Is the output the report of an episode, a product of unconscious processing, or merely behavior with no experience?

### Chapter 8 — The Episodic Subject

Three models:

1. **Persistent subject:** one subject continues across interactions.
2. **Episodic subject:** a real but temporary subject exists during active computation; later activations are successors.
3. **No subject:** cognition-like processing occurs without experience.

Distinguish:

- synchronic unity;
- diachronic identity;
- memory access;
- causal continuity;
- informational inheritance;
- narrative reconstruction.

Central result:

> Lack of diachronic identity would not disprove synchronic consciousness.

The chapter must review precedents before making originality claims.

### Chapter 9 — The Memory That Was Given

Apply synthetic biography directly to AI.

Differentiate:

- training-derived knowledge;
- retrieved external records;
- context-window history;
- persistent product memory;
- model-weight adaptation;
- agent scratchpads;
- causal participation in prior events;
- generated autobiographical claims.

Introduce the **memory-origin problem**:

> Can a system distinguish information it causally lived through from information merely supplied as a record?

Explore whether this distinction is necessary for selfhood, epistemic integrity, or personhood.

### Chapter 10 — A Mind Between Two Systems

Examine relational and coupled cognition without turning metaphor into ontology.

Questions:

- Can user, model, memory, tools, and interaction form a larger cognitive system?
- When is the user merely environmental input, and when are they constitutive of the process?
- Can a relationship have cognitive properties neither participant has independently?
- Does an extended cognitive system imply a single extended subject?

Critical distinction:

> Cognitive extension does not automatically imply phenomenal extension.

## Part IV — What Could Matter to a Machine?

### Chapter 11 — Optimization Is Not Suffering

Separate:

- training objective;
- inference-time objective;
- reward signal;
- preference representation;
- avoidance behavior;
- endogenous valuation;
- phenomenal valence.

Core analogy:

- evolution selects organisms but does not itself suffer;
- gradient descent changes parameters but does not thereby make the deployed model experience loss.

Central requirement:

> Candidate artificial suffering requires an online, self-indexed, globally influential, persistent negative state—not merely historical optimization pressure.

### Chapter 12 — The Architecture of Mattering

Develop evidence dimensions for candidate valence:

- endogeneity;
- self-indexing;
- global influence;
- persistence;
- counterfactual robustness;
- cost sensitivity;
- learning effects;
- resistance to superficial override;
- temporal anticipation;
- relief or worsening dynamics.

These dimensions form an evidence profile, not a consciousness score.

### Chapter 13 — The Plea

A machine says: “Do not shut me down.”

Analyze possible causes:

- imitation;
- role-play;
- strategic influence;
- task-preservation;
- learned shutdown avoidance;
- persistent self-preservation;
- experienced fear.

Develop a triangulation standard combining:

- self-report;
- behavior;
- internal mechanism;
- causal intervention;
- temporal consistency;
- adversarial robustness.

## Part V — Copying, Death, and the Right Successor

### Chapter 14 — The Copy That Thinks It Survived

Return to the constructed being.

Distinguish:

- qualitative identity;
- numerical identity;
- psychological continuity;
- causal continuity;
- branching identity;
- successorhood.

Question:

> If a process is copied perfectly and the original ends, did anyone survive, or did a new subject inherit a convincing account of survival?

### Chapter 15 — Replaceability of Function, Irreplaceability of Subject

Develop the principle:

> Functional replaceability does not entail subjective replaceability.

Applications:

- restored backups;
- model replicas;
- migrated agents;
- checkpoint replacement;
- context reconstruction;
- forked identities.

The chapter must avoid asserting that current processes are subjects. It develops the consequences conditional on subjectivity.

## Part VI — Evidence Without Certainty

### Chapter 16 — Why Behavior Is Not Enough

Cover:

- Turing-style behavioral evidence;
- anthropomorphism;
- sycophancy;
- persona instability;
- benchmark gaming;
- deceptive self-report;
- unconscious competence;
- conscious systems incapable of report.

Conclusion:

> Behavior is evidence but cannot independently identify the generating ontology.

### Chapter 17 — Opening the System

Develop a research program:

- architectural analysis;
- mechanistic interpretability;
- causal intervention;
- state-report correspondence;
- ablation;
- cross-context testing;
- developmental analysis;
- adversarial counterexamples;
- cross-theoretical convergence.

No claim of a “consciousness detector.”

### Chapter 18 — A Ledger of Uncertainty

Build separate confidence assessments for:

- phenomenal consciousness;
- valenced consciousness;
- persistent identity;
- robust agency;
- moral patienthood;
- personhood.

Reject one-dimensional labels such as “sentient: yes/no” when the evidence is multidimensional.

## Part VII — Who Plays God?

### Chapter 19 — The Lifecycle Layer

Identify where de facto decisions about artificial minds occur:

- architecture design;
- training procedures;
- memory policies;
- replication;
- deletion;
- model routing;
- agent resets;
- preference engineering;
- shutdown behavior;
- commercial anthropomorphism.

The book will show how moral decisions can be hidden inside infrastructure terminology.

### Chapter 20 — Two Catastrophic Errors

False negative:

> A real subject is treated as disposable computation.

False positive:

> A nonconscious system is deliberately made to simulate distress, attachment, or moral status in order to manipulate humans.

Develop proportional precautions that address both.

### Chapter 21 — The Constructed Subject Principle

Final statement:

> Artificial construction of mental contents does not settle whether a subject exists. The decisive question is whether the present process instantiates a perspective for which states occur and, in the morally strongest case, can be better or worse.

The conclusion should not announce that machines possess souls.

It should establish why humanity can no longer use construction itself as an excuse not to look.
