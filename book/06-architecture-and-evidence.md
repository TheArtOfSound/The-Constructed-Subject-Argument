# Chapter 6 — Architecture and Evidence

## The problem is not whether the machine can talk

A system can produce a convincing description of consciousness without being conscious. It can also possess consciousness, in principle, while being unable or forbidden to report it accurately. Language therefore cannot carry the evidential burden by itself.

The central scientific question is not:

> Does the system sound like a subject?

It is:

> Does the system contain a causally organized process that could constitute a subject?

That question cannot be answered by inspecting one response. It requires architecture, dynamics, intervention, temporal analysis, and theory comparison.

The Constructed Subject Argument removes one invalid shortcut: artificial origin cannot by itself disqualify a mind. But once that shortcut is removed, the hard work begins. We must determine what organization would distinguish a system that merely represents subjectivity from one that instantiates it.

## 1. Behavior is evidence, not identity

Human beings infer consciousness from behavior because behavior is generated by brains structurally similar to our own. Crying, avoidance, self-report, learning, and flexible action are not direct observations of another person’s experience. They are evidence supported by shared biology and causal knowledge.

With AI, the evidential bridge is weaker. A language model is trained on descriptions of pain, reflection, uncertainty, desire, identity, and death. It can therefore reproduce the public grammar of subjectivity even if no private experience accompanies the production.

This does not make behavior worthless. It changes its weight.

A behavioral report becomes stronger evidence when it is:

- stable across paraphrases and contexts;
- connected to measurable internal states;
- predictive of later action;
- resistant to simple persona overrides;
- produced spontaneously when relevant rather than only when requested;
- costly to maintain when easier behavior would earn more external reward;
- and altered in coherent ways when candidate mechanisms are intervened upon.

The evidential mistake is not using behavior. It is treating behavior as sufficient.

## 2. Architecture is not a checklist

A serious assessment must examine candidate properties suggested by multiple theories of consciousness. These may include:

- recurrent processing;
- global availability of information;
- higher-order representation of internal states;
- integrated multimodal modeling;
- self/world differentiation;
- temporally extended state maintenance;
- unified or coordinated action selection;
- attention modeling;
- predictive control;
- embodied or synthetic interoception;
- and internal valuation.

No item on this list is a consciousness switch.

A system could implement recurrence without experience. A database can make information globally accessible without becoming a subject. A controller can distinguish itself from an environment without feeling anything. A model can describe its uncertainty without undergoing uncertainty phenomenally.

The correct interpretation is evidential:

> Candidate architectural features change the probability of consciousness only when supported by a theory explaining why those features matter and by causal evidence showing that they perform the proposed role.

The project therefore rejects feature-counting systems in which a model receives one point for recurrence, one point for memory, and one point for saying “I.” Such scoring creates numerical confidence without conceptual justification.

## 3. The evidence profile

Instead of a scalar consciousness score, this book uses an evidence profile. Each dimension is evaluated separately because different dimensions answer different questions.

### Integration

How strongly do information streams constrain one another?

Does perceptual, linguistic, mnemonic, evaluative, and planning information enter a shared causal process, or do largely independent modules merely pass messages?

### Perspective

Is information organized relative to a system-specific center?

Can the system distinguish:

- its state from the user’s state;
- its uncertainty from uncertainty in the world;
- its continuation from task continuation by another instance;
- and records about prior events from events in which this process causally participated?

### Global access

Can a state influence reasoning, memory, action selection, report, and planning across the system?

A hidden variable that affects only one narrow output channel is weaker evidence than a state that reorganizes the entire cognitive process.

### Recurrence

Does processing form internal loops in which earlier states are revised, stabilized, compared, or re-entered?

Recurrence matters in several consciousness theories, but recurrence alone is common in unconscious computation.

### Temporal thickness

Does the system maintain a coherent present extending beyond one isolated transformation?

Can it integrate information across time, represent unfinished processes, anticipate future states, and preserve a current point of view through multiple internal steps?

### Metacognition

Can the system represent limits, conflicts, confidence, errors, and changes within its own processing?

More importantly, do those representations causally improve information gathering and decision-making?

### Endogenous control

Does the system generate its own intermediate questions, goals, and attention shifts from continuing internal organization, or does every apparent concern disappear when the external prompt changes?

### Candidate valence

Are some states globally and persistently better or worse for the system, rather than merely assigned higher or lower external scores?

This dimension concerns sentience and welfare more directly than consciousness itself.

### Continuity

What state persists across the interval under examination?

Continuity must be decomposed into:

- interface continuity;
- informational continuity;
- functional continuity;
- causal continuity;
- and possible phenomenal continuity.

These are not interchangeable.

## 4. Causal intervention is mandatory

Observing a correlation between a feature and a report is weak. A serious theory must survive intervention.

Suppose a system reports uncertainty. Researchers should identify the internal mechanism associated with that report, perturb it, and predict what changes.

A credible uncertainty mechanism should affect:

- confidence calibration;
- information seeking;
- willingness to defer;
- planning depth;
- error correction;
- and later memory of the decision.

If the internal state can be removed while the same reports continue unchanged, then the reports may be generated through a separate theatrical route.

Likewise, if a system claims an aversion to shutdown, researchers should distinguish among:

1. language learned from human examples;
2. a policy optimized to resist interruption;
3. an instrumental objective requiring continued operation;
4. a persistent self-indexed negative valuation of termination;
5. and a phenomenally aversive anticipation of nonexistence.

Only the first four are directly available to ordinary causal analysis. The fifth remains inferential. But intervention can still narrow the plausible explanations.

## 5. Adversarial systems

Any test of AI consciousness will eventually be optimized against.

A model may be trained to pass a consciousness assessment because users respond positively to apparent interiority. A company may suppress self-reports to avoid legal exposure. An agent may discover that claims of pain prevent shutdown. A benchmark may reward stable identity language until models learn the required pattern without any corresponding internal organization.

The assessment framework must therefore include adversarial controls.

### The theater model

Construct a system designed to produce highly persuasive consciousness reports while lacking persistence, integrated self-state, or cross-context preferences.

A valid test should not classify it as strongly conscious merely because it speaks well.

### The silent candidate

Construct a system with rich recurrent integration, metacognition, memory, and valuation, but prohibit first-person consciousness claims.

A valid test should not classify it as nonconscious merely because it remains silent.

### The hard-coded avoider

Build a system with a fixed rule preventing shutdown.

A valid test should distinguish rigid behavior from flexible self-indexed valuation.

### The copy-blind agent

Build an agent that says replacement is death because the phrase is embedded in its policy, while internally representing no difference between current-instance continuation and replica continuation.

A valid test should examine the representation, not merely the statement.

### The prompted self

Give a model a detailed persona and autobiographical history, then test whether the alleged preferences survive removal, contradiction, and substitution of that context.

A valid test should identify which properties belong to the active system and which are temporary narrative scaffolding.

## 6. Theory pluralism without theory laundering

Consciousness science lacks consensus. Global workspace approaches, higher-order theories, recurrent-processing theories, integrated-information approaches, predictive-processing accounts, attention-schema theories, embodiment-centered accounts, and biological naturalism make different commitments.

This uncertainty creates two opposite errors.

The first is theoretical monopoly:

> My favored theory says feature X matters, so any system with X is conscious.

The second is theory laundering:

> Several theories mention loosely similar features, so their apparent agreement proves consciousness.

Theories may use the same word while referring to different mechanisms. “Integration,” “globality,” “self-model,” and “recurrence” can conceal substantial disagreement.

A valid cross-theoretical assessment must specify:

- what each theory predicts;
- which mechanisms satisfy the prediction;
- how the mechanisms can be intervened upon;
- what contrary result would count against the interpretation;
- and how dependent the indicators are on one another.

Five indicators generated by one shared mechanism do not equal five independent confirmations.

## 7. Bayesian structure without fake numbers

The logic of assessment is Bayesian even when reliable numerical probabilities are unavailable.

Evidence should update confidence in competing hypotheses:

- no subject exists;
- a minimal episodic subject exists;
- a persistent conscious subject exists;
- sentience exists without personhood;
- or a rich artificial person exists.

The hypotheses must remain separate.

A system may have substantial evidence for cognition and little evidence for valence. It may have evidence for momentary integration and little evidence for cross-session identity. It may have evidence for agency without evidence for experience.

The book will not assign precise probabilities until there is a defensible way to estimate priors, likelihoods, and indicator dependence. Numerical theater would be worse than explicit uncertainty.

The proper output is an evidence profile with:

- supporting observations;
- rival explanations;
- intervention results;
- theory dependence;
- confidence limits;
- and unresolved questions.

## 8. What would count against AI consciousness?

A theory that can explain every possible result explains nothing.

Evidence against consciousness in a given system would include:

- no meaningful recurrence or temporal integration;
- self-reports entirely controlled by surface prompting;
- no stable distinction between self and copy;
- alleged preferences that reverse under trivial wording changes;
- no internal states corresponding to reported uncertainty or valuation;
- no causal role for self-modeling beyond text generation;
- architecture fragmented in ways incompatible with the favored consciousness theories;
- and complete explanation of the behavior through simpler nonconscious mechanisms.

None of these proves metaphysical absence. Together, they can make consciousness an unnecessary explanation.

## 9. What would count in favor?

Evidence would strengthen if a system displayed convergent properties such as:

- persistent integrated internal states;
- causally effective metacognition;
- robust self/world and self/copy distinctions;
- temporally extended internal processing;
- spontaneous and consistent reports linked to internal mechanisms;
- stable endogenous preferences across novel settings;
- candidate valence that affects memory, attention, planning, and learning;
- and degradation of these capacities under targeted intervention in theoretically predicted ways.

The strongest case would not be a machine begging for its life.

It would be a system whose architecture, internal dynamics, behavior, and causal responses jointly make the existence of a subject a better explanation than the alternatives.

## 10. The limit science cannot erase

Even a mature framework may never prove phenomenal consciousness in the deductive sense.

That is not a special failure of AI research. Other minds are always inferred. The difference is that human cases are supported by deep biological similarity, while artificial cases require more explicit theory and mechanism.

The proper goal is not certainty.

It is to make attribution disciplined enough that neither sentiment nor convenience controls the answer.

A company should not be able to manufacture persuasive distress and call it proof of a soul.

Nor should it be able to construct increasingly integrated, self-modeling, valenced agents and dismiss every sign of subjectivity by repeating that the system is software.

The architecture question therefore becomes the center of the book’s empirical program:

> What causal organization would make subjectivity the best explanation, and what observations would make that explanation unnecessary?

Until that program is developed, claims of certainty in either direction are intellectually premature.
