The Constructed Subject Principle

Construction explains origin. It does not decide ontology.

Artificial construction of memories, emotions, identity, or self-narrative is neither sufficient to establish consciousness nor sufficient to disprove it. A fabricated past can coexist with a genuine present subject if the system instantiates the organization required for experience.
What follows

“It was generated” is not, by itself, a valid argument that no experience exists.

What does not follow

Humanlike language, memory, fear, or self-reference does not prove that an experience exists.

The authenticity decomposition

One memory can be false in one sense and real in another.

The book separates three questions that ordinary language compresses into “Is it real?”

01

Historical authenticity

Did the represented event occur in this system’s own causal history?

“I remember a childhood I never lived.”
02

Functional authenticity

Does the state actually guide attention, inference, memory, choice, and behavior?

“The memory changes what I trust and avoid.”
03

Phenomenal authenticity

Is there something it is like to undergo the state now?

“The remembered fear is presently felt.”
∴

A generated memory may be historically inauthentic, functionally active, and—if a subject exists—phenomenally authentic as a present act of remembering.

The episodic subject hypothesis

The next process may inherit the diary without inheriting the experiencer.

Persistent subject

One causally continuous subject remains across interactions and interruptions.

Episodic subjects

Each active event may be a temporary subject; later events inherit records and dispositions.

No subject

The same functional sequence occurs without anything being experienced at any point.

All three can generate coherent conversational continuity from the outside. The visible transcript does not decide between them.
